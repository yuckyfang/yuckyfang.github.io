Web AR Drawing App

(Case Study in Progress)

Scenario

This is a NSF funded research project for building educational technology tools and curriculum that combines STEM, culture, and art. My professor, Ron Eglash, wanting to combine his drag-and-drop cultural coding tool (CSDT) with physical crafts, decided to focus on Augmented Reality to bridge the two. We set out to create a drawing app for the web where students can trace cultural designs and 3D models they coded online onto paper crafts, body art, and textiles with AR.

Time Period

June - August 2020

Since this app will be used remotely during COVID-19, we made sure to design it so students would understand how to draw with it without physical guidance from someone else in the room. We also provided a feature where they can collaborate with their family members.

Design Process

Understanding Target Audience -> User Interviews -> Market Research -> UX of AR Research -> Ideate -> Mockup -> Usability Testing -> Prototype -> Usability Testing -> Iterate -> Key Takeaways -> Future Improvements

Target Audience

"Students" was a very broad target audience and we didn't want to create a tool with just assumptions about our users so we narrowed down to middle school and high school students as the majority of teachers and students using this tool are in those grades.

User Interviews

With the target users in mind, I invited 5 middle school and high school students, had them test existing mobile AR drawing apps and communicate their thoughts about them, while I observed their reactions. We learned that:

  • 1. They want control over the opacity of the drawings they're tracing over
  • 2. It's tiring and inconvenient to hold up the phone with one hand while drawing with the other
  • 3. They want a tutorial to introduce them to drawing with AR
  • 4. AR often lost tracking on white paper which caused resonating negative feelings about the drawing experience

Market Research

To learn more about different AR drawing apps on the market, I utilized competitive analysis to gauge how AR drawing apps are designed and developed, their distinct features, what the users look for and expect, as well as what users like and dislike about their experiences.

UX for AR Research

Since AR is an emerging technology and one that is heavily dependent on the experience, I made sure to research lots about UX for designing AR applications. I read many AR Design guidelines from Google, Microsoft, Unity, Magic Leap, Apple, SnapChat, and Facebook as well as watched conference videos and talks about the evolving design. I created a webpage on my findings here.

Ideate + Quick Mockups

After the research, observations, and interviews, we came up with several mockups for our AR drawing app. One of the school activities was to code a 3D model of a wigwam then build a paper model from the design to learn about Native American architecture. The first picture below shows what we imagined the experience would look like after the students coded their models and wanted to see their creations in AR. It displays a character who onboards the user with a mini tuturial on how to scan surfaces and informs the users when the environment is difficult to track. There's also a "Trace This" text to visually communicate what the users will do in the activity. It's 3D so you definitely can't trace it on paper without a 3D pen but we wanted to get the students excited and intrigued about leaving with a physical craft after they complete the activity.
