Batik Bot - Research on Cyborg Textiles

Scenario

With the goal of bringing more STEM opportunities to Detroit,my professor, Ron Eglash, and I have been developing an educational program as well as a suite of design tools( CSDT ) that teaches locals how to code designs found in their culture and traditional crafts work. We hope for an end product in which locals can translate their coded designs onto textiles using the batik bot we will create out of e-waste.

Skills: C++, Arduino, SNAP, Mechanical Engineering

The second picture on the left is a prototype of our bot so far.

Cultural traditions in weaving, basketry, and other crafts support skilled labor native to African communities, but are often threatened by competition from factory-made fakes and technology made to deskill labor. This research project aims to empower traditional craft labor by designing generative technology for human-machine collaboration. We’re designing software and an educational program that teaches locals how to create traditional textile designs with code, along with developing devices that can use digital fabrication to apply the code, without the alienation that comes with mass production. This will allow older and younger generations to work together; fulfilling the elders’ desires to pass their traditions down, and the youth’s desire to be forward-looking. Currently, we are creating a robot that will assist in batik, a wax resist fabric dyeing process, made by upcycling e-waste out of old printers and scrap materials found in local communities. The robot design will be based on indigenous traditions using polar coordinate systems, and the dye itself will be made using local plant extracts to replace artificial dyes. We hope for an end product in which locals can translate their coded designs onto textiles and incorporate skilled labor in a technologically advanced world. Overall, the project hopes to set an example for merging traditions with computational systems, encourage more people to repurpose waste into useful innovations, and to put the power of science and technology innovation in the hands of underserved communities.

Checkers Playing Robot with Computer Vision & AI

For my final project, my group and I wanted to focus on the importance of upcycling e-waste so we built a robot out of an old 3D printer and programmed it to play checkers with another human using Computer Vision and AI. I led the Computer Vision side and used OpenCV to detect the color and shape of the checker pieces, figured out where they were on the board, then fed their positions to an open source AI that calculated the next best move using Python. Unfortunately, we did not finish the project before our senior year ended but you can still check out the code on my Github!

Web Game on Food Waste

In our daily lives, we readily waste resources, particularly food, by disposing of items before they are completely used. We want to raise awareness of the importance of carefully using our resources, specifically helping those who are less fortunate. By doing so, we are educating our audience about food waste and showing new ways that they can help. In the game, the player makes a fast-food order and must eat everything in a very short amount of time by clicking as quickly as possible. As the player orders more, the difficulty of finishing all of the items increases. It is easy to buy lots of food, especially at a fast food restaurant, where even a “full meal” wouldn’t cost very much. The difficulty of the game represents how it is possible to get carried away and buy more food than one would need. In many cases, unfortunately, leftover food is thrown away. In the end of our game we have a “Solutions” page that shows three ways on how they can be more resourceful and a map that shows food banks and homeless shelters in New York City.

PadPals - Menstruation Initiative for Homeless Women

Started and led an initiative with my friend to stop the stigma around menstruation and give the neccessary menstruation products every homeless woman deserves. We have collected as well as donated over 1500 products to homeless shelters across NYC through collaborations between numerous high schools. After talking to homeless women ourselves, we found they are often forced to repeatedly use old cloths or plastic bags during their menstruation process, resulting in many infections that may arise due to bacteria that live in the blood. They felt helpless as they were shamed for undergoing a natural process of their body because people around them don’t understand their circumstances. With PadPal, we hope to offer women an equal opportunity to live an affordable, comfortable, and hygienic life.

    June 2017 - Present

Autonomous "Spatula"

Throughout my high school senior year, I participated in the STEP Robotics competition for students in low income families and I learned a lot by leading my team's mechanical and programming side. The objective of the competition was to build a robot that can autonomously knock down stacks of cubes, then pick up ones with the assigned color back to our territory. At first, we brainstormed innumerous designs and created several prototypes that did not hold the load of the cubes, but we took parts of each design that worked and stringed it together for the robot we have now, the "Spatula". Since I knew how to program before joining the team, I took the responsibility to teach everyone how to code and CAD. We learned to construct a robot from scratch, utilize sensors and gears, synchronize the mecanum wheels for omnidirectional drive, and implement PID control programming. Even though we did not win the competition, I'm glad I garnered new STEM skills, communication skills, and the value of teamwork under my belt.

  • Skills: C, RobotC, AutoCAD, Mechanical Engineering with Vex

    February 2018 - June 2018
